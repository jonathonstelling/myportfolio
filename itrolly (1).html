iTrolly

iTrolly

We worked with a company called ADM Automation Limited, located in Gateshead, United Kingdom. Our design challenge was to create a trolley which would work with the iCart, an automated guided vehicle, in moving a box from one part of a warehouse/production line to the other, the only constraint was that the trolley couldn’t contain any electrical mechanisms.

About the iCart

The iCart is a compact but powerful automated guided vehicle that can automatically determine the route it should follow to deliver or collect materials and product from a variety of locations around production and distribution facilities. The iCart has been designed to work in most manufacturing and production line environments or indeed any application involving multi-point delivery and pick up. The compact size of the unit, only 1 metre long and the same width as a PC keyboard, enables it to travel down narrow gangways accessing areas that cannot be reached by forklift trucks. Versatility and simplicity are key features of the iCart. It can pick and drop trolleys around stations totally automatically. A range of smart trolleys are also available, including some that incorporate powered roller conveyors to transport industry standard plastic bins directly to and from workstations. A key feature of iCart is the Smartcatch, Enabling coupling and decoupling of trolleys without operator involvement. A simple pin arrangement on each trolley allows automatic connection and disconnection to the iCart. This allows high utilisation of iCarts as there is no need for them to wait for operators to load and unload within the delivery cycle. Operators are also free to move trolleys to the point of use. A pair of trolleys can act as a moving KANBAN system.

Concepts

We then started to think of different ways we could move a box on and off the trolley by using no electrical components. Some ideas included using the force from the wheels or from pushing into the bench, to using the weight from one box to cause another box to be pushed from the trolley.

Final Design

Here shows the final CAD model of our prototype.Due to the overall size of the trolley being reduced by around 50% compared to the initial prototypes the mobility of the trolley has been increased; this along with the use of fully rotating wheel on the trolley allow it to fully follow the path set out by the iCart. The decrease in size means less components, therefore less material used and lower the price of the trolley further. Although the trolley can only load and unload from one side, the addition of fully rotating wheels mean that the iCart can attach to the trolley from either the front of back, allowing it to make contact with tables from different sides. Mechanism is simplified from a ‘H’ to a ‘T’, containing only 25% of the moving components in comparison to the initial prototypes, allowing the process of unloading the box to be much more efficient and reliable as parts won’t be tangled. The ‘T’ is made of only two aluminium Bosch extrusions; the uses of these extrusions mean it can be easily repaired and damaged when necessary. The majority of the moving components are stored on the underside of the trolley, stopping a person from possibly being caught within the mechanism. Extrusions on the top of the trolley (front and back) will stop a box from falling off when manoeuvring around the workshop. The use of extrusion on the bottom which will store empty boxes, didn’t need any further material to be used as the slight height difference of the acrylic sheet and the Bosch extrusion form a small barrier.

Savings
